The story

The question nobody asked.

Every Swiss childhood holds two things in its hands. A piece of chocolate, broken off the bar, softening in a coat pocket on the walk home from school. A wedge of cheese cut thick at the end of a long day, eaten over dark bread without a plate.

Both, the whole country agrees, are perfect.

They have lived in the same country, on the same shelves, in the same fridges, for two hundred years. Almost never in the same bite.

Osanna is the woman who finally asked why.

She makes her pralines by hand, in Switzerland. The work is slow. Each piece is shaped, filled, dipped and finished one at a time. By the time a box is closed, every praline inside it has passed through her hands.

What she ended up with is something that didn't exist before her: eleven flavours that pair Brie, Truffle Brie and aged Parmesan with white, milk, dark and ruby chocolate. The boxes hold six of them, or twelve. Each praline is small. Each one is a complete idea.

The first time you bite into one is the moment you finally understand the question.

"Osanna is super passionate, and you can see it reflecting in her pralines."
Vanessa, My Memories

The pairing

Two halves of the same country.

Brie is air and butter. Parmesan is salt and time. White chocolate is sugar that has almost forgotten it was once cocoa. Dark chocolate is cocoa that refuses to forget. None of them is shy on its own. Together they argue, then agree, then argue again, all in the space of one bite.

Cold first, the cheese leads. Ten minutes on the counter and the chocolate steps forward. The same praline, twice.

Most people pause after the first one. Some people get quiet. Some people pass the box around without saying anything, which is the highest compliment a Swiss table can pay.

Mochito is the one that converts the doubters: Parmesan, white chocolate, mint, a quick slap of lime. It tastes a little like a mojito and a cheeseboard met somewhere in between. Tartufo, white or dark, is the one for the chocolate-only crowd. StrawBrie Ruby is the praline that always disappears first.

Eleven flavours in total. Other chocolatiers seem to think the question was worth asking, too.

"As a fellow chocolatier I strongly recommend L'Osanna Pralines. The distinct tastes and flavours are a perfect match. Masterpieces."
Merle S., chocolatier

What's in it

Swiss ingredients. One hand.

Made in a Swiss home kitchen from ingredients you can name. No preservatives, no thickeners, no shortcuts. Hand-tempered, hand-filled, hand-finished, hand-boxed.

Ingredients

Swiss Brie, aged Parmesan, Truffle Brie. White, milk, dark and ruby Swiss chocolate. Real fruit (strawberry, raspberry, orange). Mint. Lime. Nothing else hiding in the back.

Each praline is shaped and dipped individually. Tempered at temperature, by hand.

Good to know

Contains dairy. Some pralines contain nuts. Made in a home kitchen that also handles wheat and eggs. Cross-contamination is possible.

Best within a few weeks of pickup. Keep refrigerated. Take out 10 minutes before eating for the full second flavour.

How to eat them

A small ritual, not a snack.

Two temperatures, two pairings, four moments. The praline does not stay the same praline twice in a row.

i.

Cold first

Straight from the fridge. The cheese leads. Brie, Parmesan, Truffle, all forward.

ii.

Then warm

Ten minutes on the counter. Now the chocolate steps forward and the cheese turns into a soft, savoury background.

iii.

With wine

A Pinot Noir, a chilled rosé, or a glass of Swiss Chasselas. Pair it like a cheeseboard.

iv.

With coffee

An espresso after dinner. The bitterness lifts the sweet edge. The savoury notes do their best work.
