The story

A grove chosen by science. A family that already knew.

Most olive oil starts with a tree someone inherited. PNOE started with a map.

In 2023, the family spent months reading a Greek academic study called Mapping the Climatic Suitability for Olive Groves in Greece. They were looking for the conditions that turn an olive into an oil worth bottling: the right altitude, the right soil, the right slow rhythm of sun and sea wind. The map kept pointing to one corner of the Peloponnese. The region of Ilis, on the Ionian coast, west of Olympia.

That is where they bought the land.

By 2024 they were on it. Preparing the soil, walking the rows, learning the trees one at a time. The first harvest came late that autumn. Cold nights, warm afternoons, the sea about an hour away in every direction. By 2025 the project had a name: PNOE, pronounced pno-ay, the Greek word for breath.

That is the part most people miss. Olive oil is not pressed. It is breathed. Air, salt, time, hands that do not hurry. The PNOE family bottles all four into the same litre.

"Not a random land. A scientifically selected one. Because great olive oil starts from the land."
PNOE, on choosing Ilis

The land

An hour from the Ionian. A breath above the rest.

The grove sits in Ilis, in the western Peloponnese. The Ionian Sea breathes over the trees from one side. The mountains behind hold the cold air. The result is the kind of mild, ventilated climate that an olive tree would design for itself if it could.

Two things follow. The trees stay healthy without unnecessary treatments. The breeze and the dryness do most of the work that chemicals do elsewhere. And the olives ripen at their own pace, never rushed, which is what gives the finished oil its unhurried, layered taste.

The sea breeze does the spraying. The sun does the timing. We mostly stay out of the way.

The harvest happens in late autumn, by hand. Each batch of olives goes to the press within hours of being picked. Not days, not weeks. Cold extraction, no heat, no shortcuts. What you taste in the bottle is what the tree gave that morning.

The 2025 / 2026 harvest was sent to IC CHEM, an accredited laboratory in Patras, for the full chemical and organoleptic panel. It came back as extra virgin on every line.

More on the family, the land and the harvest at pnoe-project.com.

How to use it

It is not a cooking oil. It is the dish.

Four moments in a week, four ways to let this oil do its job. The first one is always the same: open the bottle, smell it, then pour a spoonful onto a piece of bread.

i.

On bread

Toasted sourdough. A pinch of salt. Pour generously. The oil is the topping.

ii.

On tomatoes

Ripe summer tomatoes, oil, salt, oregano. The four most honest ingredients of the Mediterranean.

iii.

On the salad

The last thing you add, never the first. Greek style: cucumber, tomato, feta, olives, oil. No vinegar fighting back.

iv.

On almost anything

Soup. Hummus. Roasted vegetables. A boiled egg. A finished pasta. Anywhere a final spoonful would lift the plate.
