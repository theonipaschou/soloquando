The story

The name alone is worth knowing.

In 19th-century Athens, an ergolavos was not a contractor in the modern sense. It was slang for a persistent suitor. A man who had taken on the project of winning someone. The word came from a real incident around 1855, when a group of young men kept walking past a young woman's window every evening on Ermou Street in Athens. When the neighbours asked what they were doing, they said they had taken the contract for the road. It became a court case. The slang stuck. And the pastry shop on that same street started selling these little almond cookies to those same young men. The name moved from person to pastry and never moved back.

My grandmother made them for my father. Not for occasions, just for him, the way you bake something for someone who loves it without needing a reason. She would make them on Sunday morning while the house was still quiet, and by the time everyone sat down, the apricot jam smell had spread to every room. My father ate them with his coffee. He would hold them for a moment before biting, the way you do with something you know is good.

I started making them because I missed that smell. I still make them for the same reason.

The almond

Three ingredients.
One very old idea.

Almonds, icing sugar, egg whites. That is the whole recipe. No butter, no flour, no thickener. The almond does everything: the structure, the flavour, the texture that gives a little before it gives a lot.

In Greece, almonds have meant something at every important moment — weddings, baptisms, new beginnings. They are given as gifts at celebrations because they carry the idea of happiness that takes time.

Italian nuns were making versions of this same cookie in the Middle Ages. The same three ingredients, the same method, the same result. When Catherine de Medici married the King of France in the 16th century, she brought the recipe to Paris. The French spent the next few centuries turning it into something elaborate and photographable. The Greeks kept it soft, chewy, and paired with jam, and called it after a flirt on a street corner in Athens.

The almonds in this batch are blanched whole almonds, ground fine with the sugar until the paste is smooth and slightly warm. The mixture rests in the fridge for two to three hours. That rest is not optional. It is what makes them soft in the middle and just set on the outside. If you skip it, you get a different cookie. A lesser one.
